Visas & Immigration

In an effort to prevent the spread of COVID-19, the government continues to improve and innovate, one of which is by creating digital-based public services. Recently, the Indonesian government through the Ministry of Law and Human Rights of the Republic of Indonesia launched an electronic visa service (e-Visa) which aims to provide convenience in providing services to the public, especially in pandemic conditions like today. In addition, the implementation of this e-Visa service also brings a positive message for prospective foreign tourists who want to visit Indonesia after the pandemic ends.


By providing easy access for prospective tourists, it is hoped that the development of Indonesia's tourism and creative economy will also increase after the pandemic, of course accompanied by preparing safe tourism destinations.With this service, Foreign Citizens (WNA) no longer need to come directly to the Indonesian Representative abroad or the Directorate General of Immigration to take care of, pick up, or stick visa stickers on passports. Through the e-Visa service, foreigners who intend to enter Indonesia now only need to apply for a visa online.

How to Apply for Your Visa

Here are the steps to apply for an e-Visa:


  • Open the page https://visa-online.imigrasi.go.id/
  • Register
  • Input data and upload personal data requirements (individual/corporate)
  • Wait for the notification with username and password
  • Apply for Visa Approval
  • Log in to the application using the username and password sent to you
  • Select the type of visa to be applied for, input data, and upload required documents
  • Make PNBP payment (make sure the data input and requirements are correct, because if rejected the payment cannot be withdrawn)
  • If approved, you will get a notification via e-mail

The e-Visa application can be done wherever the applicant is. The verification process will be carried out faster, namely 2 (two) working days, the visa will be sent to the applicant. In order to cut the bureaucracy, both the applicant and the guarantor will no longer meet physically, but will be sent directly via electronic mail. If foreign nationals have received an e-Visa, they can immediately travel to Indonesia. Well, this ease of access is one of the points to support the development of tourism in Indonesia after the pandemic. With this innovation, it is hoped that prospective tourists who intend to visit Indonesian tourism destinations after the pandemic will also increase.


Time Difference

Indonesia spans multiple time zones, given its vast geographical extent. The country follows three main time zones: Western Indonesia Time (WIB), Central Indonesia Time (WITA), and Eastern Indonesia Time (WIT). Western Indonesia Time (WIB) is the standard time for the western part of Indonesia, including the capital city Jakarta and Bintan Island. WIB is UTC+7 hours. Central Indonesia Time (WITA) is observed in the central region of Indonesia, including Bali, Makassar, and Sulawesi. WITA is UTC+8 hours. Eastern Indonesia Time (WIT) is used in the eastern part of Indonesia, encompassing regions such as Papua and the Maluku Islands. WIT is UTC+9 hours. It's important to note that Indonesia does not observe daylight saving time, so the time difference remains consistent throughout the year. However, some regions in Indonesia may have slight variations due to local customs or regional preferences. When traveling or scheduling events in Indonesia, it's advisable to consider the specific time zone of the area you'll be in to avoid any confusion or inconvenience.



Currency

When visiting Indonesia, it's important to familiarize yourself with the local currency and understand how to handle your finances during your stay. The official currency of Indonesia is the Rupiah (IDR), which is issued and controlled by the Bank of Indonesia. This article will provide useful information on Indonesian currency, where to buy and exchange it, and the accessibility of ATMs throughout the country.


Understanding Indonesian Currency

The Indonesian Rupiah is denoted by the currency symbol Rp and has the currency code IDR. By law, all transactions within Indonesia must be conducted in Rupiah. The daily exchange rate can be found in newspapers, through online sources, or mobile apps. It's worth noting that credit cards are widely accepted in hotels, large restaurants, and major stores, making it convenient for travelers.

Indonesian banknotes come in denominations of IDR1000, IDR2000, IDR5000, IDR10000, IDR20000, IDR50000, and IDR100000. Coins in circulation include IDR1000, IDR500, IDR200, IDR100, and IDR50. Ensure you have a mix of smaller denominations for ease of transactions, especially in rural non-tourist areas.



Where to Buy and Exchange Currency

Foreign currency can be exchanged at banks and money changers in major cities throughout Indonesia. Tourist resorts generally have money changer facilities, but if you're traveling to more remote areas, it's advisable to exchange your money beforehand. For larger currency amounts, it's best to place an order at your bank a day in advance, especially if you don't have a foreign currency account.

Plug & Electricity

When traveling to Indonesia, understanding the country's electricity standards and connectivity options is essential for a smooth and convenient stay. This article provides insights into the electrical voltage, plug types, charging facilities, Wi-Fi accessibility, and obtaining local SIM cards to help foreign visitors stay connected and prepared during their trip.


Electricity in Indonesia

The standard voltage in Indonesia is typically 230 V, with a frequency of 50 Hz. If you are from the UK, Europe, Australia, or most parts of Asia or Africa, your electronic devices can be charged using the standard voltage. However, travelers from the United States, Canada, and most South American countries may need a voltage converter due to the voltage difference. It is advisable to consider purchasing a voltage converter before your trip to ensure compatibility and prevent any potential risks.

Plug Types

Most power plugs and sockets in Indonesia follow type C and F, which are the standard European plugs with two pins. If your home country uses a different plug type, it is recommended to carry an adapter. Adapters can be easily purchased from online retailers like Amazon or local minimarkets throughout Indonesia.

Charging Facilities

Charging facilities are readily available in most restaurants, tourist attractions, hotels, airports, and public transportation in Indonesia. You can find sockets to charge your gadgets, and many places also offer USB ports for convenience. It is advisable to carry your own charging cables and adapters to ensure compatibility with the available charging facilities.





Wi-Fi and Connectivity in Public Space

ATMs connected to international networks like Plus/Cirrus or Alto are easily accessible in major Indonesian cities and tourist destinations. However, be aware that withdrawal limits may depend on your home bank. ATMs typically dispense IDR50,000 or IDR100,000 denomination notes, as indicated on the machine. Keep in mind that larger notes can be more difficult to split, particularly in rural non-tourist areas. To ensure you have cash on hand, it's recommended to withdraw money from ATMs in major cities before venturing into more secluded destinations.


Understanding the currency of Indonesia is essential for a smooth and hassle-free travel experience. The Indonesian Rupiah (IDR) is the official currency, and it is widely accepted throughout the country. Familiarize yourself with the banknotes and coins, and consider exchanging foreign currency at banks or money changers in major cities. Utilize ATMs, which are accessible in tourist areas, but be mindful of withdrawal limits and note denominations. By being prepared and knowledgeable about Indonesian currency, you can confidently manage your finances during your visit to this beautiful archipelago.


Obtaining Local SIM Cards

For reliable and continuous connectivity, obtaining a local SIM card is a practical option. SIM cards from providers such as Telkomsel, XL, Smartfren, 3/Tri, Axis, and Indosat/IM3 can be found at airports and various mobile provider booths. Prices start from IDR 10,000. Foreign visitors will need to present their passport or relevant identification (such as KITAS or KITAP for long-term stays) to register the SIM card, and the provider's staff will guide you through the registration process.

Understanding the electrical standards, plug types, and connectivity options in Indonesia ensures a seamless experience for foreign visitors. By carrying the necessary adapters or voltage converters, utilizing available charging facilities, and staying connected through Wi-Fi or local SIM cards, visitors can easily stay connected and navigate the country. Embrace the convenience of technology while immersing yourself in the rich culture and breathtaking beauty that Indonesia has to offer.

People & Culture

Indonesia, with its diverse population and rich cultural heritage, offers a fascinating experience for foreign visitors. As you embark on your journey to this archipelago, understanding the different ethnic groups, religious practices, and cultural values will enhance your appreciation of the Indonesian people. This article aims to provide insights into the people and culture of Indonesia, as well as offer guidance on proper manners and respect during your visit.


Ethnic Diversity in Indonesia

Indonesia is home to over 200 ethnic groups, residing across its 13,400 islands. The population of approximately 200 million comprises various ethnicities, each with their own languages and dialects. While the Javanese and Sundanese are the most populous groups, numbering around 70 million and 30 million respectively, there are also smaller communities residing on remote islands. Inter-marriages among different ethnic groups since gaining independence in 1945 have contributed to a more cohesive Indonesian nation.



Religious Practices

The dominant religion in Indonesia is Islam, with a majority of the population adhering to this faith. However, Bali stands out as an island where Hinduism is prevalent. In other regions, such as the Minahasa in North Sulawesi, the Toraja highlands in South Sulawesi, and parts of Papua, the majority are either Catholics or Protestants. Indonesian society, in general, is religious in nature, and people hold their beliefs with reverence and respect.


Tolerance and Unity

The Indonesian people embrace the principles of Pancasila, which advocate belief in the One and Only God, a Just and Civilized Humanity, the Unity of Indonesia, Democracy through unanimous deliberations, and Social Justice for all. These principles shape Indonesian society to be open, tolerant, and respectful of different religions, customs, and traditions. While upholding their own beliefs, Indonesians demonstrate unity in diversity, as reflected in the national motto: "Bhinneka Tunggal Ika" meaning "Unity in Diversity."


Proper Manners and Respect


As a visitor, it is important to show respect and cultural sensitivity during your stay in Indonesia. Here are a few guidelines to follow:


  • Dress modestly, particularly when visiting religious sites or rural areas.
  • Greet locals with a smile and a polite greeting, as this gesture is highly appreciated.
  • Seek permission before taking photographs of individuals or religious ceremonies.
  • Be mindful of local customs and traditions, such as removing shoes before entering homes or temples.
  • Avoid public displays of affection, as they may be considered inappropriate in certain areas.
  • Engage in conversations with an open mind, showing genuine interest in the local culture and customs.
  • By embracing the cultural diversity and demonstrating respect for the Indonesian people and their traditions, you will forge meaningful connections and create lasting memories during your visit to this remarkable nation.

Indonesia's multicultural society, encompassing various ethnicities and religions, offers a captivating experience for foreign visitors. By understanding the diverse cultural landscape, practicing proper manners, and showing respect for local customs, you can immerse yourself in the rich tapestry of Indonesian culture. As you explore this land of unity in diversity, embrace the warmth and hospitality of the Indonesian people, and treasure the enriching encounters that await you during your journey.
